01  Brand Voice & Tone
The app has a distinct personality. Every screen, notification, and error message should sound like the same person — a knowledgeable, warm, ADHD-smart friend who happens to know a lot about women's health.

The 4 Voice Dimensions
Dimension
We Are
We Are Not
Example
Warmth
Encouraging, safe, like a trusted friend
Cheery, forced, corporate-friendly
"Good to see you" not "Welcome back, user!"
Clarity
Direct, simple, one idea at a time
Clinical, jargon-heavy, complex
"Your estrogen is rising" not "Serum estradiol levels are ascending"
Intelligence
Respectful of user's intelligence, evidence-based
Talking down, oversimplifying, hedging everything
"Research suggests" not "some say" or "experts believe"
Honesty
Truthful about uncertainty, never overpromises
Alarmist, sensationalist, confident when unsure
"This is common in ADHD. We don't fully know why yet."

The Voice Test: Read It Aloud
Before finalizing any copy, read it aloud. If it sounds like a doctor's appointment waiting room sign, rewrite it. If it sounds like a guilt trip, delete it. If it sounds like your smartest, most supportive friend — ship it.

02  Language Rules

Words We Always Use
Use This
Why
"Logged"
Past tense confirms action. Feels complete, not in-progress.
"This week" / "Today"
Anchors user in time — critical for ADHD time blindness
"Your body" / "Your cycle"
Ownership language reinforces self-knowledge and agency
"This is common"
Normalizes ADHD and women's health experiences immediately
"You can always come back"
Removes pressure — ADHD users abandon when pressured
"Here's what we know"
Honest framing. Sets up evidence without overpromising.
"Try this" / "One thing"
Singularity. ADHD brains need one clear action, not a list.
"Research suggests" / "Studies show"
Credibility without false certainty
"Notice" / "Pay attention to"
Gentle observation — not alarm
"Skip this for now"
Exit ramp reduces anxiety and builds trust

Banned Words & Phrases
Never Write This
Write This Instead
Why It's Banned
"You missed your log"
"Ready when you are"
No shame for non-use — ADHD users abandon apps that guilt them
"Don't forget to..."
"When you get a chance..."
"Don't forget" triggers RSD and shame in ADHD
"You should..."
"One option is to..."
Prescriptive language raises defensiveness
"Warning"
"Just a heads up"
"Warning" triggers cortisol response — too alarming
"Alert!" / "Urgent"
"Something to notice"
Urgency is a known ADHD trigger — avoid unless truly critical
"Failure" / "Failed"
"Didn't go through — try again"
Failure language causes immediate emotional shutdown
"You haven't logged in X days"
"We missed you"
Guilt-counting causes abandonment
"Simple" / "Easy" / "Just"
(remove the word)
Minimizing language feels condescending to users who struggle
"Complete your profile"
"Add to your health picture"
"Complete" implies deficiency — reframe as building
"Premium" / "Pro" (features)
"Full access" / "Unlock"
"Premium" signals you're lesser without it
"Crazy" / "Insane"
(delete entirely)
Ableist language — harmful to mental health users
"Your ADHD brain"
"Your brain" or "this is common with ADHD"
Don't define the user by their diagnosis

05  Content Card Copy
Health cards are the core product. They must teach clearly, earn trust with evidence, and end with one action. Maximum 3 sentences in the preview. No exceptions.

Card Copy Formula
Content Card Formula (4 Elements)
	•	HOOK (1 sentence): The most interesting or useful fact. Present tense. Active voice.
	•	CONTEXT (1–2 sentences): Why this matters for this user. Connect to ADHD or women's health specifically.
	•	ACTION (1 sentence): The single most useful thing they can do with this information.
	•	EVIDENCE TAG: Evidence: High / Medium / Low  ·  Source: [Institution, Year]

Card Copy Examples

Example — Cycle Phase Card
Element
Copy
Hook
Your focus sharpens during the follicular phase — that's days 1–13.
Context
Rising estrogen supports dopamine production. For ADHD brains, this often means clearer thinking and easier task-starting.
Action
Try scheduling your hardest tasks in days 8–12 of your cycle.
Evidence tag
Evidence: High  ·  Source: Douma et al., Psychoneuroendocrinology, 2024

Example — PMDD Card
Element
Copy
Hook
PMDD affects 3–8% of women — but it's much more common in those with ADHD.
Context
The exact rate is disputed, but research consistently shows ADHD and PMDD co-occur more than chance. Progesterone sensitivity and dopamine fluctuation are likely involved.
Action
Track your mood in the 7–10 days before your period to start seeing your personal pattern.
Evidence tag
Evidence: Medium  ·  Source: Doyle et al., Journal of Attention Disorders, 2023

Evidence Level Definitions
Level
What It Means
Copy Used
High
Multiple peer-reviewed RCTs or systematic reviews. Consensus in field.
Evidence: High
Medium
Observational studies, small RCTs, or emerging research. Promising but not definitive.
Evidence: Medium
Low
Case reports, anecdotal, expert opinion, or single small study
Evidence: Low — Emerging Research
Traditional
Eastern medicine, functional medicine practice not yet in RCT evidence
Traditional Practice (not RCT-tested)

Copy Anti-Patterns for Content Cards
✗  NEVER WRITE THIS
Studies show that estrogen plays a key role in the regulation of dopaminergic neurotransmission, which may have implications for attention deficit hyperactivity disorder symptom expression across the menstrual cycle.
✓  WRITE THIS INSTEAD
Your estrogen peaks mid-cycle — and for ADHD brains, that often means a clearer, more focused few days.

✗  NEVER WRITE THIS
WARNING: Low progesterone in the luteal phase may cause severe mood symptoms in some patients. Consult your physician.
✓  WRITE THIS INSTEAD
The week before your period, progesterone drops. For many women with ADHD, that's when focus gets hardest. That's real — and it's hormonal, not a personal failing.

Notification Rules
Non-Negotiable Notification Rules
	•	Maximum 1 push notification per day — never send 2+ in 24 hours
	•	All notifications require opt-in — never send without permission
	•	Never send urgency language ("Last chance", "Expires", "Don't miss")
	•	Never mention streak counts in notifications — that's FOMO pressure
	•	Never send notifications after 9PM or before 7AM — respect sleep
	•	Always offer notification preferences in-app — easy to access
	•	Test notification copy by asking: would a good friend send this text? If no, rewrite.

Button Copy Rules
CTA Writing Rules
	•	Always start with a verb: Log, See, Get, Start, Track, Read
	•	Maximum 4 words per button label
	•	Never use: Submit, Okay, Continue (too corporate or vague)
	•	Never use: Update, Proceed, Confirm without context (too clinical)
	•	Destructive buttons (delete, remove) must be explicit — never hidden as 'Continue'
	•	Skip / dismiss options must be visually present — never hidden or grey
	•	Primary button copy changes meaning per screen — never reuse generic labels

Achievement Copy Rule
Never use exclamation points in achievement copy. 'You did it!!!' feels hollow and performative. A calm, genuine acknowledgment lands more strongly with ADHD users who have been over-praised and then abandoned by other apps.

12  Paywall & Subscription Copy
The subscription offer must be honest, clear, and low-pressure. ADHD users are especially sensitive to FOMO manipulation and dark patterns — one instance of that destroys trust permanently.
